Drexel One AI Chatbot

Inclusive AI-powered UX research for international students

UX Research Lead · Academic Research Project

International students at Drexel were frustrated with the university portal. I spent months interviewing them, running usability tests, and figuring out what was actually going wrong. The biggest surprise? Translating the interface made things worse, not better.

Context & Constraints

  • Grant-funded academic research project with live international student participants
  • Cross-functional research, design, and development team
  • Balanced research depth with limited development resources
  • Users with varying language proficiency and accessibility needs

Problem

Students kept getting lost in the portal. They couldn't find what they needed, gave up on tasks, and felt stupid - even though the problem wasn't them. It was the system.

Role & Cross-Functional Collaboration

Role: UX Research Lead (Qualitative). Collaborated with designers, developers, faculty, and students. Research findings influenced design direction, evaluation criteria, and iteration priorities.

  • Led UX research strategy; secured funding and coordinated with design and development
  • Designed multilingual and accessibility-focused studies
  • Synthesized qualitative and behavioral data

Research Methods

  • Interviews and task-based usability testing (qualitative-led)
  • Surveys and A/B tests supported evaluation across multilingual flows
  • Identified key barriers; designed AI-assisted support concepts; prioritized cross-cultural clarity over direct translation

Scope: 30 interviews, 50 A/B tests across multilingual task flows.

What I learned

This one surprised me: translating the interface word-for-word made students more confused, not less. The terms didn't carry the same meaning in different languages. We had to explain concepts, not just translate words. That insight changed everything about how we approached the redesign.

How Research Informed Decisions

  • Insight: Direct translation increased confusion. Decision: We deprioritized literal translation and invested in concept-based explanations and AI-assisted guidance (D.A.V.E.) to reduce cognitive load.
  • Insight: User workflow mapping showed deep navigation and unclear labeling caused task failure. Decision: We simplified information architecture and prioritized cross-cultural clarity over information density.
  • Testing → change: A/B testing across multilingual flows validated that the new approach improved task success and satisfaction; we iterated on copy and flow based on behavioral data.

Impact

+80% user satisfaction
+89% task success rate
Improved comprehension for Farsi-, Spanish-, and Chinese-speaking users

What stayed with me

AI can help people feel less lost - but only if you actually understand what's confusing them in the first place. The technology isn't the hard part. Listening is.
