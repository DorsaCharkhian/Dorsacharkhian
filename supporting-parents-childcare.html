Supporting Parents in Childcare

Trust-centered service design for non-emergency pediatric decision support

Role: UX Researcher (Qualitative, applied) · Team Project

When a kid has a fever at 2am, parents panic. They Google symptoms, call family, spiral. We talked to parents about what would actually help them feel less scared - and it wasn't another symptom-checker app.

Context & Constraints

Academic team project in healthcare UX. Focus on non-emergency pediatric decision support; concepts validated through research synthesis and team critique, not deployed product metrics.

Problem

Parents don't trust the random health advice they find online. They're anxious, overwhelmed, and just want someone to tell them "it's fine" or "go to the doctor." But getting that reassurance is surprisingly hard.

Role & Cross-Functional Collaboration

Role: UX Researcher (Qualitative, applied). Collaborated with designers, researchers, and stakeholders. Research findings influenced concept selection (hotline, mobile clinic, companion app), service touchpoints, and trust-centered evaluation criteria.

  • Qualitative research on parent decision-making, trust drivers, and stress points
  • Interview design, synthesis, and insight extraction
  • Journey mapping and service flows to surface trust and clarity breakdowns
  • Information architecture and content structure for service touchpoints
  • Low- and mid-fidelity prototypes in Figma and Canva

Research Methods

  • Semi-structured interviews with parents and caregivers
  • Empathy mapping and journey mapping
  • Thematic synthesis (trust, credibility, accessibility)
  • Scenario-based evaluation of non-emergency pediatric decisions

Synthesis was thematic and iterative; concepts validated through team critique and alignment with research themes (conceptual outcomes, not deployed metrics).

What I learned

Parents didn't want more information - they wanted a person. Someone who could listen, understand the context, and say "here's what I'd do." That changed our whole approach from building a content library to designing human connections.

How Research Informed Decisions

  • Insight: Parents trusted medical professionals over fragmented online sources; high uncertainty in non-emergency decisions.
  • Implication: Solution needed to center credible human support and reduce cognitive load, not add more generic content.
  • Decision: Team converged on trust-centered service concepts (24/7 hotline, mobile clinic, companion app) -access to professionals and clear next steps over feature breadth.

Design Concepts (Team Prototypes)

The team explored service concepts informed by research:

  • 24/7 Parent Hotline - Phone and text access to nurses and pediatric professionals
  • Mobile Pediatric Clinic - Pop-up clinics for underserved urban and rural areas
  • Companion Mobile App - Appointment context, location, and medical history intake

Concepts prioritized accessibility, clarity, and trust over feature complexity.

Impact (Conceptual Outcomes)

What stayed with me

Sometimes the best design isn't a feature. It's access to a real person who can help.
